Evaluating Optimal Farm Management of Phosphorus Fertilizer Inputs with Partial Observability of Legacy Soil Stocks
with Zachary S. Brown, David M. Kling, Luke Gatiboni, Justin S. Baker

Phosphorus (P) is an essential element for all living things, including agricultural crops. Modern farming systems are reliant on P fertilizers produced from depletable stocks of phosphate mineral rock. However, synthetic P fertilizer applications in such systems is believed to often be higher than what should be the economically efficient level for farm profits. Over-application causes environmental damage through nutrient losses and eutrophication in surface water. Phosphorous loss can be mitigated through on-farm management interventions, including alternative application rates and use of riparian buffers. Unlike nitrogen, however, a portion of P applied to cropping systems can bind to and accumulate in soil. This soil binding means there can be a delay between fertilizer application and surrounding water quality impacts, depending on existing levels of soil saturation. Soil-bound P can also be utilized by crops, depending on its bioavailability. This accumulated P – referred to as ‘legacy phosphorus’ – could provide a significant alternative source of P fertilization for crops, particularly in cropland that has a long history of intensive cultivation or animal agriculture. Policy incentives for greater utilization of this in situ farmland resource could also potentially provide a promising strategy for reducing agricultural runoff and water pollution. Farmers cannot directly observe utilizable legacy P stocks on their land, which makes it challenging to predict how crop yields will be affected by reducing P fertilizer application and relying on legacy P stocks. A signal of the current legacy P stock can be obtained via soil sampling. The goal of this paper is to better understand the dynamic incentives farmers face when choosing their degree of reliance on legacy P stocks each year. To accomplish this objective, we first construct a stochastic dynamic programming model of legacy P stock dynamics, crop yields, input and output prices, and profits. Treating legacy P stocks as partially observable, we then pose the farmer’s optimization problem as a Mixed-Observability Markov Decision Process (MOMDP) and use dynamic programming to compute solutions. We characterize an optimizing farmer’s demand for P fertilizer and soil sampling, given the current state of prices and beliefs about legacy P stocks, and aim to characterize farmers’ potential responses to different price-based policy incentives to reduce P application. We also use this framework to investigate the potential role of farmer risk aversion in these decisions.
